Understanding Tornado Genesis

Wind Shear

Before a tornado can form, there must be wind shear. This occurs when wind speed or direction changes significantly with height. Faster winds aloft moving over slower winds near the ground. This difference in speeds creates an invisible, horizontal tube of rotating air, similar to a rolling pin or a carpet being rolled across a floor. This is known as horizontal vorticity.

Tilting by the Updraft

A thunderstorm needs a strong updraft—a column of warm, rising air—to fuel its growth. When a powerful updraft encounters one of these horizontal "rolling pins" of air, it pushes a section of the tube upward. As the updraft lifts the middle of the rolling air, the tube tilts. This process transforms the horizontal rotation into vertical rotation. Once the rotation is vertical, it forms the core of a mesocyclone, which is the rotating heart of a supercell thunderstorm.

Stretching and Intensification

Once the rotation is vertical, it must be concentrated to reach tornadic intensity. This happens through vortex stretching. The updraft "stretches" the vertical column of air. As the column is stretched vertically, it becomes narrower. To conserve its momentum, the air must spin much faster.

The Final Descent

For the rotation to reach the ground and become a tornado, it often requires help from the Rear Flank Downdraft (RFD). This is a plume of cool, sinking air at the back of the storm. The RFD wraps around the mesocyclone and carries the vertical rotation down toward the surface. If the air in the downdraft is buoyant enough (not too cold), the rotation can maintain its strength as it reaches the ground, completing the birth of the tornado.
