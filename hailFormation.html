Understanding Hail Formation

Hail formation is a fascinating process that requires specific "ingredients" and a very powerful storm structure. Essentially, a hailstone is a piece of ice that is kept suspended in a cloud by rushing air until it grows too heavy to stay up.

The Ingredients

For hail to form, a thunderstorm needs three things:

  • A powerful updraft: A strong column of rising air that moves from the bottom of the storm toward the top.
  • Supercooled water: Liquid water droplets that are colder than 0 degrees Celsius but haven't frozen yet.
  • Freezing temperatures: The top of the storm must be well below freezing.

The Embryo Phase

It starts with a small "embryo," usually a frozen raindrop or a bit of snow (called graupel). As the updraft pushes this embryo higher into the cold parts of the storm, it collides with supercooled water droplets. These droplets freeze instantly onto the embryo on contact.

The Growth Cycle

The hailstone doesn't just fall immediately. The updraft is often strong enough to keep the ice suspended. As the hailstone is tossed around inside the storm, it continues to collect layers of ice. Clear ice forms when the hailstone is in a slightly warmer part of the cloud. The water spreads out before freezing, creating a clear layer. Opaque ice forms when the water freezes so quickly that air bubbles get trapped inside, making the ice look white or cloudy.

Falling to Earth

Eventually, the hailstone grows so large that its weight overcomes the strength of the updraft, or it gets caught in a downdraft (air moving downward). Gravity takes over, and the hail falls to the ground.
